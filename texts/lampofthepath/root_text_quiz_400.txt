200 ADVANCED QUESTIONS ON THE LAMP OF THE PATH TO ENLIGHTENMENT
(Root verses with commentary by Jamgon Kongtrul Lodro Thaye)
=======================================================

INTRODUCTION AND PRELIMINARY MATTERS (Q1-Q15)
==============================================

Q1. According to the commentary, what three key points did the scholars of Vikramaśilā assert should be given in a preliminary account before explaining a text?
a) The historical context, the doctrinal framework, and the specific meditation instructions contained therein
b) The greatness of the author, the greatness of the teaching, and how such a teaching should be heard and explained
c) The lineage of transmission, the purpose of the composition, and the dedication of merit at its conclusion
d) The biography of the translator, the circumstances of the translation, and the intended audience of the work
ANSWER: b

Q2. According to the commentary, in what specific way did Atiśa demonstrate his mastery over the qualities of scriptural knowledge?
a) He memorised the entire Kangyur and Tengyur during a single three-year retreat at Nālandā monastery
b) He composed more commentaries than any other paṇḍita at the great Indian monastic universities
c) He was able to recite by heart the complete collection of Nāgārjuna's Middle Way treatises
d) He gained complete mastery over almost the entirety of the Three Baskets of scripture
ANSWER: d

Q3. What does Naktso Lotsāwa say in his Eighty Verses of Praise about Atiśa's impact on Tibet?
a) That Atiśa brought the secret mantra teachings which had never before been known in the Land of Snows
b) That the teachings of the three vehicles were finally reconciled through Atiśa's unmatched scholarly genius
c) That before the Noble Master's arrival in Tibet, all were as blind men, and once he arrived, the sun of wisdom dawned
d) That the dharma wheel turned without ceasing from the moment of Atiśa's arrival until his passing at Nyethang
ANSWER: c

Q4. According to the commentary, what three qualities make the Lamp of the Path to Enlightenment superior to other traditions?
a) Its antiquity, its popularity among practitioners, and its endorsement by the Tibetan royal court
b) Its brevity, its logical consistency, and its systematic presentation of the four classes of tantra
c) Its reliance upon Nāgārjuna alone, its emphasis on meditation over study, and its accessibility to laypeople
d) Its subject matter is fully comprehensive, it is easy to put into practice, and it is adorned with the oral instructions of Atiśa's two teachers
ANSWER: d

Q5. What does the commentary say about the subject matter of the Lamp of the Path in the section on the greatness of the teaching?
a) It condenses only the sūtra teachings, leaving tantra to be explained in separate specialised texts
b) It presents the essential meaning of Mādhyamaka philosophy while setting aside the Yogācāra tradition
c) Since it condenses the key points of both sūtra and mantra, its subject matter is fully comprehensive
d) It focuses exclusively on the path of the superior person, omitting the lesser and intermediate paths
ANSWER: c

Q6. According to the commentary, what is the meaning of the Sanskrit word 'bodhi' in the title Bodhipathapradīpa?

c) The complete purification of the two obscurations and the complete mastery of all dharmas as they seem and as they are
d) The ultimate union of bliss and emptiness which is accomplished through the practice of highest yoga tantra
ANSWER: c

Q7. Why is the text called a 'lamp' according to the commentary's explanation of the title?
a) Because it completely dispels the darkness of lack of understanding, misunderstandings and doubts, and illuminates the nature of the ten bhūmis and five paths

c) Because it was the first composition to illuminate the meaning of the Kālacakra tantra for Tibetans
d) Because Atiśa compared the Buddha's teachings to a series of progressively brighter lamps in his auto-commentary
ANSWER: a

Q8. According to the commentary, why is the Indian title of the text stated at the beginning of the translation?
a) To demonstrate the authenticity of the source of the teaching and to generate gratitude and appreciation for the translators

ANSWER: a

Q9. Why was the translator's homage to Mañjuśrī Kumārabhūta inserted at the beginning?
a) Because Atiśa himself was recognised as an incarnation of Mañjuśrī and so the homage honours the author

Q10. According to the commentary, what did Lha Lama Changchub-O specifically request Atiśa to compose?
a) A śāstra on how to practise, which has few words, condensing the meaning of the entire Mahāyāna

c) An encyclopaedic work covering all the philosophical systems of the Indian Buddhist tradition
d) A manual for the proper bestowal of monastic ordination according to the Mahāsāṃghika lineage
ANSWER: a

Q11. What three lineages did Atiśa bring together in composing the Lamp of the Path, according to the Introduction?
a) The Sakya, Kagyu and Nyingma lineages of Tibetan Buddhism that existed before his arrival

Q12. According to the Introduction, who was Dharmakīrti of Sumatra and why was he important to Atiśa?

THE THREE PERSONS: OVERVIEW AND DEFINITIONS (Q16-Q40)
=====================================================

Q16. According to the commentary, what does the term 'person' (puruṣa) specifically signify in the context of the Three Persons?
a) Any sentient being who has taken rebirth in any of the six realms of saṃsāric existence
b) One who has taken the prātimokṣa vows and entered the monastic community of the saṅgha
c) A human being who has encountered the Buddhist teachings and received empowerment from a qualified lama
d) Possessing the ability to accomplish the benefit of future lives, or having an intellect able to discern what to accept and reject
ANSWER: d

Q17. According to the commentary, is someone whose purposes are solely concerned with this life considered a 'person' in the context of this teaching?
a) No, someone concerned only with this life is not what is meant by the terms 'person' or 'human' in this context
b) Yes, they are the most basic type of lesser person on the graduated path
c) Yes, but only if they maintain the ten virtues as a foundation for temporal happiness
d) No, unless they have at minimum taken refuge in the Three Jewels as a basis for future progress
ANSWER: a

Q18. What does the Blue Compendium quote about the activities of humans on the path?
a) That humans should practise the six perfections as the sole means to attain complete omniscience
b) That only those who take the bodhisattva vow can be considered to be practising the authentic dharma
c) That being afraid of bad rebirth they abandon nonvirtue, wishing to be free from saṃsāra they practise the three trainings, and afraid of the Hīnayāna they train in bodhicitta
d) That the five aggregates must first be understood as empty before any genuine spiritual practice can begin
ANSWER: c

Q19. According to the commentary, what two kinds of benefit are to be accomplished for beings through the dharma?
a) Temporal happiness and definitive goodness
b) The benefit of peace in this life and the benefit of a favourable rebirth in the next life
c) The benefit of individual liberation and the benefit of universal liberation for all sentient beings
d) Mundane siddhis and supreme siddhis as taught in the Vajrayāna tradition
ANSWER: a

Q20. According to the commentary's overview of the Three Persons, what two kinds of definitive goodness are there?
a) The freedom of mere liberation from saṃsāra, and complete omniscience
b) The attainment of a pure land and the attainment of the formless absorptions
c) The cessation of the defilement obscurations and the cessation of the cognitive obscurations
d) The realisation of personal selflessness and the realisation of phenomenal selflessness
ANSWER: a

Q21. What two methods does the commentary give for achieving complete omniscience?
a) The graduated path of study and the sudden path of meditation retreat
b) The path of the śrāvakas and the path of the pratyekabuddhas
c) The analytical approach of Mādhyamaka and the experiential approach of Mahāmudrā
d) The Mahāyāna of the perfections and the Mahāyāna of mantras
ANSWER: d

Q22. According to the commentary, what relationship do the paths of the lesser and intermediate persons have to the path of the greater person?
a) They are common preliminary practices and components of the training of the greater path
b) They are independent and self-sufficient paths that lead to their own distinct results
c) They are obstacles that must be completely abandoned before one enters the Mahāyāna
d) They correspond to the first and second turnings of the wheel of dharma respectively
ANSWER: a

Q23. What does the commentary cite from Śāntideva's Caryāvatāra about the moment bodhicitta arises?
a) That the person will be called an 'heir of the Sugatas' and be praised by gods and men
b) That all the Buddhas of the ten directions rejoice and make offerings to the new bodhisattva
c) That the three worlds tremble and the gods of the desire realm weep with joy
d) That all their previously accumulated negative karma is immediately and completely purified
ANSWER: a

Q24. According to the commentary, what is essential for being counted as a Mahāyānist?
a) Having received the four empowerments of the highest yoga tantra from a qualified vajra master
b) Having studied and memorised the major treatises of both Nāgārjuna and Asaṅga
c) The generation of supreme bodhicitta—if this intention is abandoned, one leaves the Mahāyāna community
d) Having completed at least one three-year meditation retreat under the guidance of an authentic lama
ANSWER: c

Q25. What does the commentary describe as the specific means and methods of the lesser person's path?
a) Practising the six perfections while maintaining awareness of dependent origination at all times
b) Taking full monastic ordination and engaging in extensive study of the Abhidharma literature
c) Making vast material offerings to the Three Jewels and performing prostrations and circumambulations
d) Having developed conviction in karmic causes and results, adopting moral discipline of abandoning the ten nonvirtuous actions, and cultivating conditioned meditative absorptions
ANSWER: d

Q26. According to the commentary, what are the four successive dharma cycles that comprise the main practices of the path of the lesser person?
a) Taking refuge, training in loving kindness, cultivating compassion, and generating bodhicitta
b) Study, contemplation, meditation, and realisation of the nature of mind
c) Offerings to the Three Jewels, confession of misdeeds, rejoicing in merit, and dedicating virtue
d) Reliance on the teacher, contemplating impermanence and the faults of the lower realms, taking refuge, and training in the moral conduct of abandoning the ten nonvirtues
ANSWER: d

Q27. According to the commentary, what three principles characterise the intermediate person?
a) The contemplative principle of turning away from conditioned happiness, the practical principle of maintaining prātimokṣa precepts, and the principle of the result in seeking nirvāṇa for oneself
b) The principle of study, the principle of contemplation, and the principle of meditation
c) The principle of renunciation, the principle of ethical conduct, and the principle of wisdom
d) Faith in the Three Jewels, conviction in karma, and disenchantment with saṃsāric pleasures
ANSWER: a

Q28. How does the commentary further classify intermediate persons according to Sharawa's teaching?
a) By their monastic rank—novice monks, fully ordained monks, and abbots of great monasteries
b) By the three types of meditation they practise—calm abiding, analytical meditation, and resting meditation
c) By the Four Noble Truths: turning away from conditioned happiness is the truth of suffering, nonvirtuous actions is the truth of origination, one's own peace is cessation, and desisting is the path
d) By their geographical location—those in India, those in Tibet, and those in other regions of Asia
